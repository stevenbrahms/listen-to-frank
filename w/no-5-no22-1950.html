NO. 5/NO. 22

MARK ROTHKO, 1950 (dated on reverse 1949)

By 1950, the rectangles have locked in. But this one still has the scaffolding up. Get close to the red band. Three thin white lines run through it, wobbling like a pulse. Within a few years, details like that are gone and the surfaces go shut. Right here, he's still deciding. Now say something nice about the colors. Go ahead. It would have made him furious. In 1956, a writer named Selden Rodman got Rothko talking for a book of interviews with artists. Rothko told him flat out: I'm not an abstractionist. I'm not interested in the relationship of color to form. I'm interested only in expressing basic human emotions. Tragedy. Ecstasy. Doom. The colors weren't the subject. They were the actors. Rothko read a ton of Nietzsche, and he talked about these paintings less like abstractions than tragedies. Color instead of script. In his mind, it was the same job as the Greeks: put you in a room with death, love, terror, all the big unbearable stuff, and give you nowhere else to look. Watch what the red is doing to the yellow in here. That's the play. So don't stand here matching the orange to your couch. That is exactly the kind of compliment he couldn't stand. Then he dropped this on him. People break down and cry in front of these pictures, he told Rodman. Not moved. Not touched. Crying. And the weepers, he said, are having the same religious experience he had when he painted them. Now sit with how strange that is. He was dead certain this worked. He'd watched it work. But ask him how. How colored mud on a piece of cloth reaches into a stranger and turns something over. He had nothing. No formula, no trick to hand you. The man was surest of the one thing he could never explain. The painting does its job on people who can't say why. Made by a man who couldn't say how. So don't stand here naming the colors. Stand here and check if it's working on you.
