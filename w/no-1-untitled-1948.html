NO. 1 (UNTITLED)

MARK ROTHKO, 1948

It's 1948, and look what's left of the world. Nothing here has a name. A green patch. A blue brick. Some black scribbles. Orange burning through a tan fog. Try to point at one thing and say what it is. You can't. By now, Rothko has decided something that sounds nuts: almost everything is in the way. The people. The objects. The strange little creatures he used to paint. All of it standing between him and you. He called what he wanted clarity. Nothing between him and the idea. Nothing between the idea and you. Here's the logic. Give your brain something to name. A face. A boat. An apple. Watch it pounce. It names the thing, files it, moves on. You've read the picture before you've felt a thing. Take that away. Nothing to name, nothing to solve. So whatever this painting is going to do to you, it does it before the thinking starts. So he starts subtracting. The creatures melt into this. Floating patches people later called Multiforms. And here's the man himself, right there in the surface. Thin washes of paint soaking into the canvas, one over another. Green under blue. Orange pushing through tan. Until the light looks like it's coming from behind the canvas instead of bouncing off it. Nothing about this is casual. You can feel him looking, changing something, looking again. So this isn't a guy who can't paint a face. This is a guy getting rid of everything he knows how to paint, on purpose, to see what's left. Within a year or two, these patches stop drifting. They square up. They stack. They turn into the paintings all around you. This is the last second before that. The whole world, halfway to gone.
