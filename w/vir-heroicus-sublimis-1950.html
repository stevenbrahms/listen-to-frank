VIR HEROICUS SUBLIMIS

BARNETT NEWMAN, 1950

Holy shit! Look how big this thing is. That's not a joke. That's the point. You don't ease into this painting. It takes over your vision before your brain has time to decide how it feels. So here's your first job. Step closer. Closer than feels right. When this was first shown, in 1951, Newman put a note on the wall next to it. It basically said: people look at big paintings from far away. Don't. These are meant to be seen up close. A wild instruction for a painting almost eight feet tall and eighteen feet wide. He meant it. He wanted red past the edges of your vision. Then the lines. Newman called them zips. Friendly little word. Nothing friendly about the job. The zips give you scale. Your body measures itself against them the way it measures a doorway. Without them, the red would just keep going. Which sounds peaceful, right up until you're standing in it. The title is Latin. Vir Heroicus Sublimis. Man, heroic, sublime. Not heroic like a statue on a horse. Heroic like a person standing in front of something enormous with nothing to protect them. A couple years earlier, Newman wrote an essay called The Sublime Is Now. The argument: European art spent centuries chasing beauty. American painters could chase awe instead. The storm, the cliff, the open ocean. That feeling where your regular little personality briefly stops being in charge. And when he first showed paintings like this, people thought he'd lost his mind. The show flopped. Critics were brutal. Somebody vandalized one of the paintings right there in the gallery. He was in his forties, not some kid. He kept going. So stand close. Don't try to solve it. Some people feel lifted. Some feel irritated. Some feel small and want to leave. All of that counts. The painting isn't asking what you think. It's asking how long you can stand here before you start looking for a way out.
