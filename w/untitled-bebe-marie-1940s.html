UNTITLED (BÉBÉ MARIE)

JOSEPH CORNELL, 1940s

So look at her eyes first. Get close, right up to the branches. Glass eyes, wide open, looking straight back at you. And you know this story. Girl in the thorns, whole castle asleep, somebody eventually comes crashing through the briars to wake her up. Except this girl is already awake. She's been watching the whole time. So how does she end up in this little box? You have to go to Queens. Joseph Cornell lived most of his adult life on Utopia Parkway with his mother and his brother Robert. Sounds fake, right? Utopia Parkway. But that was really the street. And then you go downstairs, and that's where his little kingdom is. Not some fancy artist studio. No beautiful north light. We're talking shelves, jars, old magazines, labeled boxes, clippings, toys, maps, hotel labels, cut-out birds, ballerinas, stars. Stuff that looked like junk until Cornell gave it another life. He'd go into Manhattan, dig through bookshops and junk shops, bring things home, and somehow, down there, the evidence turned into art. Tiny theaters. French hotels. Places he could visit without really leaving home. But this one feels different. The doll came from a family attic in Nyack, where Cornell was born. So this isn't Paris. It isn't the moon. This is childhood. And then he puts her in the woods. Twigs, dried flowers, a dead little thicket right in front of her face. But look where the branches open. Right at her eyes. He buries her, then gives you the exact place to find her. The title gives you almost nothing. Just her name in parentheses: Bébé Marie. In the fairy tale, someone wakes the girl. Here, nobody comes. Just you, standing too close to a box made by a man in a basement, staring at a girl who was never asleep. So lean in. Maybe that's the rescue.
