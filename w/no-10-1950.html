NO. 10

MARK ROTHKO, 1950

Here's a question you might be asking by now. Why does this guy get a whole room? Most artists in this building get a wall. A corner. Rothko gets the room to himself. And no, he didn't personally demand this room. But this is pretty much how he wanted these things seen. Rothko was a nightmare about installation. The paintings had to hang low. The light had to be dim. He didn't want you strolling past, glancing over, checking Rothko off the list. He wanted you stuck in there with them. Which is exactly where you are. So use it. Get closer than feels polite. Eighteen inches. That was his number. Close enough that the yellow stops being a color you're looking at and starts becoming the air in front of your face. Watch what happens at the edges. The blue pressing into the yellow. The gray underneath swallowing everything above it. Nothing in here gets to mind its own business. Now, the funny part. This exact painting was the first Rothko ever to enter this museum's collection. In 1952, the architect Philip Johnson bought it from the Betty Parsons Gallery and gave it to MoMA. And in the curator Dorothy Miller's notes, beside this painting, she wrote: "Number Ten. MoMA?" With a question mark. Fair enough. Seven and a half feet of rectangles in 1952. You can see why there was a question mark. They took the chance. Now it gets a room.
