NO. 10

MARK ROTHKO, 1950

The yellow is the trap. At first you think, okay, that's the easy part. The warm part. Stay with it. It stops being easy. White at the top. Blue under that. The big yellow. Pale gray below. Sounds orderly when you list it. In person, nothing here is orderly, because the colors don't sit still. They work on each other. The yellow opens up for a second, then the blue starts leaning on it. The gray underneath looks passive until you catch it muting everything above. Nothing gets to stay pure. The painting is basically a social situation. Now, 1952. This becomes the first Rothko to enter MoMA's collection. A gift from the architect Philip Johnson. Worth pausing on, because plenty of people looked at this and saw a con. Some bands of color? That's the painting? The museum took it anyway. One trustee was so annoyed, he reportedly resigned. Which is dramatic. But also useful. Because that tells you how strange this looked before Rothko became Rothko. The museum was right. And Rothko wanted these seen close. Uncomfortably close. Eighteen inches, he said. Which is not a viewing distance. That's the distance you stand from a person. So try it. Step in until the yellow fills everything you can see, and hold still. The colors keep working on each other. And this close, they start working on you. Then look at that yellow one more time. It's still yellow. It just isn't friendly anymore.
