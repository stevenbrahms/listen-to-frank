UNTITLED

MARK ROTHKO, 1969–1970

Almost no color left. A black field. A gray field. One hard line where they meet. And a thin white border, fencing the whole thing in. Rothko never used to give a painting an edge like that. Here's what changed. April 1968. His heart nearly kills him. An aneurysm, in the aorta. The doctors give him one rule: nothing over three feet. For a guy whose whole life's work was paintings bigger than your body, tall enough to swallow you. That's not medical advice. That's a sentence. So the giant canvases stop. What comes now is smaller. A lot of it on paper. Worked fast, because he doesn't have the strength for the old slow way anymore. And the color drains out. Black over gray. Gray after gray. He made these in a converted horse stable on Sixty-Ninth Street, where he'd hung a parachute over the skylight to choke off the daylight. Because even now, even dying, he would not let the light land wrong. People have read them ever since. Moonscapes, some said. It was the year of the moon landing. Endings, said others. Rothko said almost nothing. Here's what's known, plain. On the morning of February 25th, 1970, a truck full of his paintings reached London. The ones he made for that restaurant, then refused to sell, then gave to the Tate instead. That same morning, in that studio, his assistant found him. He'd taken his own life. He was sixty-six. Nobody gets to tell you what the black-and-grays mean. He didn't. So just look. The gray is worked, scrubbed, restless. The black holds still above it. Between them, one flat line, straight as a horizon. He spent his whole life demanding his paintings get a room of their own. Dim. Quiet. Close enough to stand inside. He never got one that stuck. You're in it. Stand as long as you need.
