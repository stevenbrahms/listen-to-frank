UNTITLED

MARK ROTHKO, 1969–1970

Almost no color left. A black field. A gray field. A hard line where they meet. And around the whole thing, a thin white border. That's new. The earlier paintings never had an edge like this. This one is fenced. In the last year of his life, this is what Rothko keeps returning to. Black over gray. Gray after gray. Canvas after canvas. People have tried to read them ever since. Moonscapes, some said. It was the year of the moon landing. Endings, said others. Rothko said almost nothing. Here is what's known, plainly. On February 25, 1970, the Seagram murals arrived in London, at the Tate. The paintings he had pulled back from the Four Seasons a decade earlier and given away instead. That same day, in New York, Rothko was found dead in his studio. He had taken his own life. He was sixty-six. Nobody gets to say what the black-and-grays meant. He never did. So just look. The gray is worked, scrubbed, restless. The black above holds still. Between them, one level line, straight as a horizon. It's the plainest painting in the room. Stand as long as you need.
