NO. 37/NO. 19 (SLATE BLUE AND BROWN ON PLUM)

MARK ROTHKO, 1958

Okay. Same Rothko, but somebody turned the lights down. Plum. Slate blue. A heavy bar of brown. Earlier in this room, these bands were running yellow and orange. Same basic setup. Completely different mood. And 1958 is the year Rothko landed the biggest commission of his life, then tried to turn it against the people paying for it. The Four Seasons. Brand-new restaurant inside the Seagram Building. All bronze and money. They offered him thirty-five thousand dollars to paint the dining room. He said yes. Then he went completely overboard. More than thirty canvases for a handful of walls. Dark reds. Browns. Black. He wasn’t trying to make the room look nice. He was trying to change what happened inside it. In 1959, on a boat to Europe, he told the writer John Fischer what he was really after. He wanted, his words, to ruin the appetite of every son of a bitch who ever ate in that room. A decorating job. Turned into a trap. Then, according to the version everybody loves, Rothko and his wife Mell finally went to the restaurant. One look at the room, the people, the whole expensive machine of it, and he pulled out. Returned the advance. Kept the paintings. This canvas isn’t one of the murals. But it’s what his hand was doing that same year. Going dark. Going heavy. A guy hired to make a room look better, trying to make the room turn on its owners.
