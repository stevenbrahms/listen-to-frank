NO. 37/NO. 19 (SLATE BLUE AND BROWN ON PLUM)

MARK ROTHKO, 1958

The lights are going down in this one. Plum. Slate blue. A bar of brown. Earlier in this room, these bands ran yellow and orange. Same setup. Dimmer room. And 1958 is the year Rothko landed the biggest commission of his life, then tried to turn it against the people paying for it. The Four Seasons. Brand-new restaurant inside the Seagram Building. All bronze and money. Thirty-five thousand dollars to paint the dining room. He said yes. Then he went all in. More than thirty canvases for seven spots on the walls. Dark reds. Browns. Black. He wasn't trying to decorate the room. He was trying to change the air in there. In 1959, on a boat to Europe, he told John Fischer from Harper's what he was really doing. He wanted, his words, to ruin the appetite of every son of a bitch who ever ate in that room. A decorating job. Turned into a trap. Then he and his wife Mell finally went to the restaurant. Or at least, that's how the story goes. One look at the room, the people, the whole expensive machine of it, and he pulled out. Returned the seven-thousand-dollar advance. Kept the paintings. This canvas isn't one of the murals. But it's what his hand was doing that same year. Going dark. Going heavy. A guy being paid to decorate a room, and trying to make the room fight back.
