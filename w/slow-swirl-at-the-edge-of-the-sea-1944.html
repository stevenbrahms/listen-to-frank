SLOW SWIRL AT THE EDGE OF THE SEA

MARK ROTHKO, 1944

Look at this thing. Two strange little beings at the edge of the sea, all curls and stems, like they're performing a ceremony nobody remembers the words to. This is a Rothko. Yeah, that Rothko. The glowing-rectangles guy. Before the rectangles, there was this. His Surrealist phase. Myth stuff. Half-human forms. Little biomorphic weirdos, washed up from the unconscious. Now, 1944. He's past forty. Barely known. Recently divorced. Living on very modest means. Twenty years of painting, and almost nobody cares. And that year, he meets Mary Alice Beistle. Everybody calls her Mell. Not long after, they're married. So people have always looked at these two figures, swaying at the edge of the water, and read them as the two of them. Him and Mell. Mid-courtship. He never said so. But the year is the year, and it's hard to look at this and not see a couple, circling each other, deciding. And the painting never really left the family. Decades later, his son Christopher said it was the first painting he could remember. It hung over the couch, in the living room of the family brownstone. Before it was an important early Rothko, it was just the thing over the couch. One more look. Squint a little. A pale field on top. A dark band along the bottom. The creatures are standing on one horizontal stripe, under another. Eventually, he takes the creatures out. Keeps the stripes. Keeps the hovering. Keeps that feeling that something enormous is happening and nobody can quite say what. The rest of that story is hanging one floor down.
